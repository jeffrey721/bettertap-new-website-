What NSF/ANSI 42, 53, 401 and P473 actually mean

By The Better Tap Team•4 min read

Shopping for a water system means running into a string of numbers — NSF/ANSI 42, 53, 401, P473 — that can feel like a code only engineers understand. The good news is that each number stands for a specific, testable promise. Once you know what they verify, the labels become genuinely useful instead of intimidating.

Why certification matters at all

NSF International is an independent organization that writes standards for water treatment products. When a system is certified to an NSF/ANSI standard, it means a third party tested it and confirmed it does what it claims. In other words, the performance is verified by someone other than the company selling it.

The four numbers, decoded

  • NSF/ANSI 42 covers aesthetic effects — the things you notice. This is about reducing chlorine taste and odor so the water is pleasant to drink.
  • NSF/ANSI 53 covers health effects. This addresses contaminants with a potential health impact, such as lead and certain other substances.
  • NSF/ANSI 401 covers emerging contaminants — the newer concerns like trace pharmaceuticals and chemicals that turn up in modern water supplies.
  • NSF/ANSI P473 is the protocol specifically focused on PFOA and PFOS, two of the well-known PFAS "forever chemicals."

Reading a label with confidence

A system certified across all four standards is making a broad, verified claim: it improves taste, addresses health-related contaminants, tackles emerging concerns and targets specific PFAS compounds. That is a far stronger position than a vague "purifies your water" promise with nothing behind it.
