What's really in your tap water — and what to do about it

Most tap water in the U.S. is treated to be safe to drink. But "safe to drink" and "what you'd choose to drink" aren't always the same thing. Here's a clear, no-scare-tactics look at what can travel through your pipes — and how a good filter changes the glass in your hand.

Treated at the plant isn't the same as clean at the tap

Your local utility treats water before it leaves the facility. The catch is the journey afterward: water can pick up traces of metals like lead from older service lines and home plumbing, plus chlorine and other byproducts left over from disinfection. None of that shows up by looking at the glass.

The contaminants worth knowing

A handful come up again and again in water reports: lead, chlorine and its byproducts, PFAS ("forever chemicals"), microplastics, and trace pharmaceuticals and hormones. They're usually present at low levels — but many people would rather not drink them at all, especially with kids in the house.

Why we keep the minerals

Here's where filtration gets interesting. Reverse-osmosis systems strip nearly everything out — including the calcium, magnesium and potassium that make water taste good and do your body good. BetterTap's MAZE filtration is built to do the opposite: remove what's harmful while keeping the healthy minerals in. Cleaner water that still tastes like water.

A simple next step

Start by reading your local water-quality report — then decide what you want out of your glass. If "remove the bad, keep the good, and skip the bottled-water runs" sounds right, that's exactly what BetterTap is for.
