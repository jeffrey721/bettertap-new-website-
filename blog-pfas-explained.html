PFAS, explained: the 'forever chemicals' in everyday water

By The Better Tap Team•5 min read

You may have seen the phrase "forever chemicals" in the news and wondered what it actually means for the water in your kitchen. The chemicals in question are called PFAS, and the nickname is more than a headline — it describes a real property worth understanding in plain language.

What PFAS actually are

PFAS is short for per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, a large family of man-made chemicals first developed decades ago. They became popular because they shrug off heat, grease and water, which made them useful in a long list of everyday products.

Why "forever"

The same toughness that made PFAS handy is exactly what makes them stubborn in the environment. They resist breaking down naturally, so once they are made they can linger for a very long time. That persistence is why people started calling them "forever chemicals."

How they reach the tap

PFAS have been used so widely that traces can find their way into soil and water supplies over time. Common sources connected to the family include:

  • Nonstick cookware coatings.
  • Stain- and water-resistant fabrics and carpets.
  • Grease-resistant food packaging.
  • Certain firefighting foams used in the past.

Because PFAS do not break down easily, even small everyday uses can add up across a region and eventually touch a water source.

What you can do about them
